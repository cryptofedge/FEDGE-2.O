# Sync Licensing Guide — TV, Film, Netflix & Beyond

## What is Sync Licensing?
Sync (synchronization) licensing = getting paid when your music is used in TV shows, films, Netflix, ads, video games, YouTube channels, etc. 
Two licenses are always needed:
1. **Master license** — controls the recording (owned by the artist/label)
2. **Sync license** — controls the composition/publishing (owned by songwriter/publisher)

As an independent artist, Mando owns BOTH — which is actually an advantage. He can negotiate and move faster than signed artists.

## Step 1: Get His Catalog Ready for Licensing

Before pitching, every song needs:

**Metadata (non-negotiable):**
- [ ] Song title, artist name, songwriter credits
- [ ] ISRC code (DistroKid generates these automatically)
- [ ] BPM and key
- [ ] Genre tags and mood tags (e.g., "energetic, urban, triumphant, street")
- [ ] Instrumental version of every song (CRITICAL — music supervisors often need vocals-off)
- [ ] Clean version if explicit
- [ ] Stems (separate tracks: vocals, beat, bass) — premium value

**Registration (required to collect royalties):**
- [ ] Register with a PRO (Performing Rights Organization):
  - USA: **ASCAP** or **BMI** (pick one, free to join)
  - Both pay performance royalties when music airs on TV/film
- [ ] Register songs with **SoundExchange** (collects digital performance royalties)
- [ ] Register with **Music Reports / Harry Fox Agency** via DistroKid or directly

Music supervisors decide what music goes in shows/films. This is the highest-value path.

- **LinkedIn** — search "music supervisor Netflix" / "music supervisor TV"

- **Film Music Network** — filmmusic.net

## Step 4: Non-Exclusive vs Exclusive Licensing

**Always push for non-exclusive** — means the same song can be licensed to multiple projects simultaneously. More money, more placements.

Only go exclusive if the fee is very high (5 figures+) or it's a massive brand/show.
